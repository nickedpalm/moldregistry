## How to Choose a Mold Inspector in Fresno

**Look for CMI or ACAC certification first.** A Certified Mold Inspector (CMI) has passed a rigorous exam and carries liability insurance. ACAC credentials (Council-certified Microbial Consultant, CMRS, CIEC) mean the inspector has additional training in remediation oversight and indoor air quality. If someone can't name their credential, they're not qualified. Full stop.

**Verify they do moisture mapping, not just eyeballing.** Visual inspections catch obvious colonies—the black stuff on drywall under a leak. But mold grows behind walls and under insulation. Credentialed inspectors use moisture meters and thermal imaging to find the wet spots before the visible damage shows up. In Fresno's dry climate, the leaks that matter are the ones you can't see.

**Ask about third-party lab analysis.** Any inspector worth hiring sends air and surface samples to an independent lab, not an in-house testing operation. The lab report should identify the mold species and spore counts. That's the data a remediation contractor (or your attorney, if this becomes a legal issue) actually needs.

**Get a written scope of work before they start.** The scope should specify: How many rooms will be inspected? Will they do moisture mapping? Air sampling (how many samples)? Surface swabs? Turnaround time for the report? What's included in the fee, and what's extra? A $250 inspection that doesn't include lab analysis is cheaper, not better. A $600 inspection with air sampling, moisture mapping, and a 48-hour lab turnaround might be the better deal.

**Pro Tip:** If you're buying a home in Fresno and the inspection contingency is tight, call the inspector three days before closing and confirm lab turnaround time. A five-day report delay kills deals. The best inspectors know their lab partners' schedules.

## What to Expect

A mold inspection in Fresno typically runs $200–$800 depending on the scope and home size. A basic visual inspection of a 2,000-square-foot house with moisture mapping is usually $300–$450. Add air sampling and surface analysis, and you're looking at $500–$800. Lab results come back in 3–7 business days (sometimes faster if you pay for expedited processing).

The inspector will spend 2–4 hours in the home, photographing areas of concern, taking moisture readings, and collecting samples if warranted. You'll get a written report with photos, findings, species identification (if mold is present), moisture source analysis, and remediation recommendations. The report is your evidence—make sure it's detailed enough to hand to a contractor or submit to an insurance claim.

**Reality Check:** Inspectors who quote you a price and then call back asking for more money because they "found something" are running a bait-and-switch. Get the full scope in writing upfront, including what triggers additional sampling or fees.
