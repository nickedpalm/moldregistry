## How to Choose a Mold Inspector in El Paso

**Look for credentials first.** A legit mold inspector should hold at least one of these: CMI (Certified Mold Inspector), ACAC CMC (Council-certified Microbial Consultant), ACAC CMRS (Remediation Supervisor), or IAC2 Indoor Air Quality certification. These aren't rubber stamps—they require study, exam passage, and continuing education. If someone can't produce a credential or describes themselves as just "experienced," walk.

**Check their conflict of interest.** This is critical: if the same company doing the inspection also does remediation work, you've got a built-in incentive for them to find (or exaggerate) mold. Reputable inspectors either don't remediate, or they're transparent about it and use a separate licensed contractor for any work. Ask directly: "Do you do both inspection and remediation?" Listen to how they answer.

**Demand third-party lab work.** A visual inspection alone isn't enough. Real inspectors use moisture mapping equipment, take air and surface samples, and send them to an independent lab (not their own in-house facility). You need that independent verification in writing. Expect them to specify which species they're testing for and why.

**Verify Texas licensing where it applies.** Texas doesn't license mold inspectors statewide, but that doesn't mean anything goes. Check the credentials above, and ask for references from local real estate agents, water damage restoration companies, or your homebuyer's attorney. In El Paso's active real estate market (especially around the newer developments northeast of downtown), your agent likely has names worth asking for.

**Pro Tip:** Get at least two inspectors' estimates. A reputable inspector will quote $300–$600 for a standard residential inspection in El Paso (bigger homes or commercial properties run $600–$800). Wildly cheap quotes suggest corners are being cut; wildly expensive ones suggest upselling. The middle range is usually right.

## What to Expect

A standard mold inspection runs 2–4 hours for a residential property, depending on size and complexity. The inspector will do a visual walk-through, check humid zones (bathrooms, basements, crawl spaces—especially relevant in El Paso's older west-side neighborhoods where plumbing leaks are common), take moisture readings, and collect samples if mold growth is suspected or air quality needs assessment. You'll get a detailed report within 5–10 business days identifying any species found, the extent of growth, likely cause (water intrusion, HVAC condensation, humidity), and remediation recommendations.

**Reality Check:** Don't confuse the inspection report with a remediation estimate. The inspector tells you *what* is there and *why*; a licensed remediation contractor (ACAC CMRS certified) tells you how much it costs to fix. Get both before signing anything.
