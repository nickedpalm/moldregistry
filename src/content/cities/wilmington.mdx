## How to Choose a Mold Inspector in Wilmington

**Look for the right credential first.** CMI (Certified Mold Inspector) is the baseline—it means they've passed a standardized exam and understand moisture mapping, air sampling, and lab protocols. Even better: ACAC CMC (Council-certified Microbial Consultant) or ACAC CMRS (Remediation Supervisor cert). These aren't just resume padding. They signal someone who's done the work long enough to pass a rigorous peer review. Ask which lab they use for samples—reputable inspectors partner with third-party labs, not ones they own. That matters for credibility.

**Verify they do visual + moisture mapping + sampling, not just one.** A thorough inspection should include a thermal camera (moisture shows up as temperature variations), air samples from problem areas *and* unaffected rooms (for comparison), and surface samples if needed. If someone quotes you $300 and promises to find all mold in 45 minutes, they're not doing the work. The job typically runs $200–$800 depending on square footage and complexity.

**Ask for references specific to your situation.** If you're buying a home, ask if they've done pre-purchase inspections in your neighborhood. If you had a roof leak, ask for someone who's handled storm damage. Wilmington's older housing stock means basement moisture and foundation seepage are routine—find someone who knows that problem cold.

**Pro Tip:** Get the estimate in writing before they start. Request that the final report includes species identification (some molds are more concerning than others), extent of growth, moisture source analysis, and specific remediation recommendations. A sloppy report is useless in court or for insurance claims.

## What to Expect

Most inspections take 2–4 hours depending on home size. Afterward, samples go to the lab (turnaround is usually 5–10 business days). You'll get a written report with photos, lab results, and next steps. Pricing ranges from $200 for a small condo to $800+ for a large house with known issues. 

**Reality Check:** Don't confuse the inspection cost with remediation. A mold inspector identifies the problem and recommends protocols. A mold *remediator* does the actual cleanup. These should be separate vendors—hiring the same company for both creates a conflict of interest (they have financial incentive to find more mold and recommend larger jobs). Get the inspection report first, then get quotes from licensed remediation contractors.
