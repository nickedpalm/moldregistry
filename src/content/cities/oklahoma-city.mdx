## How to Choose a Mold Inspector in Oklahoma City

**Verify credentials first.** Look for CMI (Certified Mold Inspector) or ACAC certifications (CMC, CMRS, CIEC). These aren't suggestions—they're the difference between someone who took a weekend course and someone who passed a rigorous exam. Oklahoma doesn't mandate state licensing for mold inspectors, which means credentials are your only reliable signal of competence.

**Ask about their process.** You want visual inspection *plus* moisture mapping and lab-verified air or surface sampling. If they're just looking around with their eyes and charging you $500, you're getting burned. Legitimate inspectors document findings photographically and send samples to third-party labs—this costs them money, which is why it matters.

**Check for insurance and references.** General liability insurance should be non-negotiable. Ask for references from real estate agents, contractors, or homeowners they've worked with in the past year. One bad reference is a yellow flag; no references at all is a dealbreaker.

**Get multiple quotes.** You'll see variation between $200 and $800 per inspection depending on square footage and complexity. Get three estimates and ask what's included in each—testing, report turnaround, remediation recommendations. The cheapest option isn't always the worst, but the vaguest one usually is.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask prospective inspectors about their experience with water intrusion events specific to Oklahoma—roof leaks after hail storms, basement moisture from heavy rain, HVAC condensation in humid months. Someone who knows your local problem set will spot issues others miss.

## What to Expect

A standard mold inspection in Oklahoma City runs $300–$600 for a single-family home, depending on square footage. The inspector typically spends 2–4 hours on-site doing visual assessment, moisture readings, and collecting samples if needed. You'll get a detailed written report within 5–10 business days that identifies mold species (if found), affected areas, likely moisture source, and next steps.

Turnaround matters here: if you're buying a house and the inspection contingency is tight, confirm upfront that the inspector can deliver results in time. Lab analysis adds 3–5 days, so factor that into your timeline.

> **Reality Check:** If someone quotes you $150 for a full inspection with lab testing, they're either losing money or cutting corners. The actual cost of lab analysis alone runs $50–$200 per sample. Suspiciously low pricing usually means incomplete work.
