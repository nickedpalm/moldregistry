## How to Choose a Mold Inspector in Wichita

**Look for third-party credentials first.** CMI (Certified Mold Inspector) through ACAC, IICRC, or similar bodies means they passed a real exam and maintain continuing education. If they also hold ACAC CMC (Microbial Consultant) or CMRS (Remediation Supervisor) certifications, that's a sign they understand remediation protocols without being incentivized to sell you expensive work. Red flag: inspectors who also own a remediation company and do their own reports—conflict of interest is baked in.

**Ask whether they do lab-verified sampling.** Visual inspection catches obvious problems, but moisture mapping (using meters) and air/surface samples sent to an independent lab tell you species, extent, and whether you're actually dealing with a toxicity issue or just cosmetic mold. Budget $100–300 of your inspection cost for sampling; skip it and you're guessing.

**Verify their local experience with Kansas humidity and construction.** Wichita gets continental humidity swings—dry winters, humid summers, and spring moisture is aggressive. A qualified inspector knows whether that discoloration in your basement is active growth or old staining from last year's leak. They've seen Wichita's typical construction (post-war brick, 1970s–80s particle board, modern vinyl) and know where moisture gets trapped.

**Check references and turnaround time.** A real inspection takes 2–4 hours depending on square footage. A report should land in your inbox within 5 business days. If someone's quoting you 45 minutes or two weeks, move on.

> **Pro Tip:** Before you hire, ask directly: "Do you perform remediation work?" If yes, ask who their lab partner is and whether they're independent. If they do both inspection and remediation in-house, get a second opinion from someone with no skin in the remediation game.

## What to Expect

A standard mold inspection in Wichita runs **$300–600 for a typical home** (under 3,000 sq ft), more for larger properties or if you add lab sampling. You're paying for expertise, not volume—a thorough inspector will spend time in crawlspaces, attics, and under-sink cabinets, not just walk through your living room.

The process: visual assessment with moisture meter readings, targeted air/surface samples if warranted, photos and notes, then a lab report (3–7 days) that identifies species, colony-forming units (CFU), and remediation guidance. You'll get a written report detailing what was found, likely causes (roof leak, plumbing, humidity), and next steps—sometimes "monitor" or "clean with a HEPA vacuum," sometimes "call a licensed remediation contractor."

> **Reality Check:** Mold remediation itself costs $800–15,000+ depending on scope. Don't let an inspector push you toward in-house remediation without getting a second opinion. The inspection and the fix should come from different people.
