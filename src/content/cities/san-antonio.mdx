# Mold Inspectors in [San Antonio](/locations/texas/san-antonio/), Texas

Finding a qualified mold inspector in San Antonio is harder than it should be. You're either staring at a Google results page stuffed with contractors who'll upsell you on remediation the moment they find a water stain, or you're reading certifications you don't understand and hoping the person who shows up actually knows what they're doing. This directory cuts through that. Below are the credentialed professionals in San Antonio who specialize in honest assessment — the kind of inspector who tells you whether you have a real problem or just a humidity issue.

## How to Choose a Mold Inspector in San Antonio

**Check for active credentials.** CMI (Certified Mold Inspector), ACAC CMC (Council-certified Microbial Consultant), or IAC2 Indoor Air Quality certification aren't optional — they're the difference between someone who knows moisture mapping from someone who guesses. Look for current renewals. Expired certs mean they stopped paying for ongoing education.

**Verify they don't also do remediation.** This is the conflict-of-interest trap. If the same company inspects *and* remediates, they have a financial incentive to find (or exaggerate) problems. A standalone inspector has no stake in the outcome — their job is accuracy, not upsell. Ask directly: "Does your company offer mold removal services?" If yes, find someone else.

**Ask about sampling and lab protocols.** Real assessments include visual inspection, moisture mapping with calibrated equipment, *and* air/surface samples sent to a third-party certified lab — not their own lab. This matters because labs they own are incentivized to report positive results. A credible inspector uses independent labs and can name the testing facility upfront.

**Get a written scope before the inspection.** You're paying for a specific deliverable: a report identifying species, extent, likely cause, and remediation recommendations. Confirm in writing what's included (visual inspection, moisture mapping, sampling, lab analysis) and what's not. A vague estimate is a red flag.

**Pro Tip:** San Antonio's humid subtropical climate and older housing stock mean plumbing leaks, roof damage, and AC condensation issues drive most mold problems. Ask the inspector if they've worked on homes built in your era and neighborhood. Experience with local building codes and common failure points matters.

## What to Expect

Inspections typically run $200–$800 depending on home size, complexity, and whether sampling is included. A 2,000-square-foot home with basic visual assessment and moisture mapping lands around $300–$500. Full sampling with lab analysis adds $200–$400. Turnaround on lab results is usually 5–10 business days.

The process itself takes 2–4 hours on-site. The inspector will document moisture levels in walls, crawlspaces, and attics, photograph problem areas, collect samples if warranted, and follow up with a written report.

**Reality Check:** Don't confuse "cheap" with "good." Inspectors quoting $150 for a full assessment are either cutting corners or planning to sell you remediation. Similarly, if the report recommends immediate, expensive remediation without explaining why, get a second opinion from someone with no financial stake in the outcome.

## Local Market Overview

San Antonio's real estate market has been competitive for a decade, and lenders increasingly require mold clearance before closing — particularly for homes with any history of water damage. That's driven legitimate demand for inspection, but it's also attracted unlicensed operators. Stick to credentials. The Texas Department of Licensing doesn't regulate mold inspection the way it does HVAC or electrical work, so your only safeguard is verified certifications and references.

The inspector you hire should be comfortable explaining their findings in plain language, not jargon designed to sound urgent. If they can't explain why a crawlspace reads 65% humidity and what it means for your home, keep looking.
