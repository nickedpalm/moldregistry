## How to Choose a Mold Inspector in Long Beach

**Verify their credentials first.** In California, the gold standards are CMI (Certified Mold Inspector), ACAC CMC (Council-certified Microbial Consultant), or ACAC CMRS (remediation supervisor). These aren't rubber stamps—they require documented training, exam passage, and ongoing education. If someone won't state their credential or abbreviates it weirdly, move on.

**Ask if they do moisture mapping and lab sampling.** A visual inspection alone is incomplete. Real inspectors use moisture meters, thermal imaging, and send air/surface samples to third-party labs (never their own lab—conflict of interest). This is how you get a defensible report that identifies species, extent, and likely cause. Expect them to explain their process before they show up.

**Check the scope for your situation.** Buying a home? You need a general building assessment. Post-flood or after roof damage? They should prioritize moisture intrusion patterns. Occupants with persistent respiratory symptoms? You need air quality testing plus visual inspection. A good inspector will ask clarifying questions on the phone—if they quote you a flat rate without asking what happened, that's a red flag.

**Request references, especially from real estate agents or contractors.** Long Beach's real estate market moves fast, and agents know who delivers credible reports on time. One solid reference beats ten five-star reviews from people who just needed to check a box.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask directly if they've testified as an expert in mold cases or been retained by attorneys. It sounds oddly specific, but inspectors who've been deposed tend to be more rigorous with documentation and sampling protocols—because they know their work will be scrutinized.

## What to Expect

A standard inspection runs $200–$800, depending on scope and property size. A basic visual walkthrough with moisture mapping sits at the lower end; adding air and surface sampling bumps you toward the higher range. Turnaround on lab results is usually 5–7 business days. You'll get a written report that identifies findings, probable causes, and remediation recommendations—this is your roadmap for what comes next (and your insurance company's reference point if claims are involved).

> **Reality Check:** If someone quotes you $150 for a full inspection, they're cutting corners. If they quote $1,500+ for a standard residential property without sampling, they're padding. Know the ballpark before you call.
