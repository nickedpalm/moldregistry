# Mold Inspectors in [Boston](/locations/massachusetts/boston/), Massachusetts

Finding a qualified mold inspector in Boston shouldn't feel like solving a crime. Yet homeowners call around, get vague callbacks, and somehow end up hiring someone who shows up with a flashlight and a clipboard—no lab testing, no credentials, no real answers. Then the report lands in your inbox and you're staring at jargon you can't verify. This directory cuts through that. Below are vetted mold inspectors across Boston who hold real credentials (CMI, ACAC certifications, IAC2), run actual sampling protocols, and send results to third-party labs so you know what you're dealing with.

## How to Choose a Mold Inspector in Boston

**Look for active, current certifications.** CMI (Certified Mold Inspector) and ACAC CMC (Council-certified Microbial Consultant) aren't decorative. They require documented training, continuing education, and liability insurance. If someone won't email you a copy of their cert, keep scrolling. Massachusetts doesn't license mold inspectors at the state level, which means credentials matter more than they should—but they do.

**Ask if they do moisture mapping and lab testing.** A visual inspection alone tells you nothing. Real inspectors use moisture meters, thermal imaging, and pull air/surface samples that get analyzed by a certified lab. If they quote you $300 flat-rate and promise answers in an hour, they're not actually inspecting—they're guessing. Expect $200–800 depending on home size and scope; labs add 5–7 business days for results.

**Check the timeline against your closing date.** If you're buying a home in Boston and your inspection contingency closes in 10 days, you need someone who can turnaround within 48 hours. Not all inspectors move that fast. Confirm upfront.

**Ask about the remediation connection.** A good inspector should diagnose the problem and recommend a protocol—but should *not* be the same company doing the cleanup. That's a conflict of interest. If they own a remediation arm, they have incentive to find more mold than is actually there. Ask how they handle that separation.

**Pro Tip:** Boston's older housing stock (many pre-1950s buildings with plaster, uninsulated basements, and sketchy ventilation) is mold-friendly. If you're buying anywhere from Beacon Hill to Jamaica Plain, water intrusion history matters more than the current inspection alone. Ask your inspector to specifically assess basement moisture and roof/gutter condition—those are your early-warning systems.

## What to Expect

A typical mold inspection runs 2–4 hours for a standard single-family home. The inspector will walk the property, note visible discoloration or water stains, check humidity levels and moisture content in suspect areas, photograph everything, and pull samples from anywhere that looks questionable. Those samples go to a lab; you'll get results within a week. The final report should name the mold species present (or absence thereof), quantify the extent, explain the likely source (roof leak, plumbing, HVAC condensation, basement seepage), and recommend next steps—which might be "hire a contractor" or "improve ventilation" or "nothing urgent."

**Reality Check:** Price variation is real. A $250 inspection often means visual only, no sampling. A $600+ inspection usually includes moisture mapping and lab work. Don't assume cheaper = bad or expensive = thorough. Ask what's included before you book. Many inspectors also offer air quality testing (measuring particulates, CO2, humidity) or mold remediation verification—those are add-ons, not standard.

## Local Market Overview

Boston's building inspector network is tight and competitive. Most major inspectors here also serve Cambridge, Brookline, and the inner suburbs. Water intrusion claims spike after nor'easters and spring thaw, so book early if you're buying in March or April. The city's older housing stock means most properties have *some* mold history—the question isn't whether to inspect, it's whether what you find is a dealbreaker or a maintenance item.
